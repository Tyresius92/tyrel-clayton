I am a first generation college student, which means that I'm the first in my family to go to college. There's a lot of hardship that goes along with that, which is frequently overlooked. For starters, many first generation college students come from low income homes, which means they have no idea how they're going to pay for college. 

I was one such student. Growing up, my parents always told me that I'd need to work hard and get lots of scholarships so that I could pay for college on my own, because they wouldn't be able to help me. 

By the time I graduated high school, I had manage to secure $4,000 a year, which added to my Pell Grants, would have supplied about $10,000 per year. Unfortuntely, that was only about 50% of the cost of going to one of the state universities. It started to look like college might be out of the question. 

And then I got a letter about the Machen Florida Opportunity Scholars (MFOS). I was given a full ride. The University of Florida wanted to invest in first generation students, and make sure they had the opportunity to succeed, and I was going to benefit.

It was a godsend.

Going to college was suddenly _real_. It was possible. I was going to be able to learn, and break my family's cycle of poverty. I was going to be able to make something of myself. MFOS changed the course of my life, and I wouldn't be the person I am today without it.

I graduated college, and started working, just living my post-college life.

Then, about five years ago, I listened to an episode of the Listen Money Matters podcast, and they talked about a study which showed that one of the most effective ways to increase your personal happiness is to give to charity. 

That made sense to me, so within a couple of days, I began donating to a few different charities, including MFOS. The amounts were small, about $20 a month to each of them. I can't say it was the most responsible decision: I had $15K in student loan debt, and $8K in credit card debt, all while making about $40K per year. 

But something about it felt _right_. It made me feel good to give back. It made me happy to know that I was helping causes that I believed in. All the claims made in the podcast were true, and even more, I was helping these organizations do good work.

Over the years, I've increased my income into the low six figures and paid off most of my debts (there are a couple low interest ones that I'm not in any hurry to pay off). I still live as though I'm making the same $40K a year, so my savings rate has skyrocketed, and for the first time in my life, I feel some measure of financial security.

But the thing that brings me the most joy about my newfound security is my ability to give a lot more to charity, and specifically to the Machen Florida Opportunity Scholars. 

Over time, my contribution increased to $400 a month to this one charity. My money was going directly to first generation college students, paying for their tuition, their housing, and their food. It was a great feeling. 

Eventually, the university contacted me. Apparently, I was the most consistent alumni donor giving to the program, and they told me that if I upped my contribution to $500 a month, I could create an endowment, and would have a far more lasting impact. Instead of directly supporting students, the money will be invested, and the gains will support students. In theory, this means that the money will last forever, and still be putting kids through college decades (or centuries!) from now.

I've committed to give $30,000 over the next five years. In exchange, the university is going to put the money in a scholarship fund named The Tyrel Clayton Opportunity Endowment. That's right: they named it after me. Talk about awesome.

This all started because I wanted to increase my personal happiness. Five years into the experiment, I can confirm: giving to charity is a great way to increase your happiness. 

But more than that - it's not really about my happiness anymore. Like, yes, being in a position to do this makes me happy. But I've realized that the more important thing is trying to give others the chance that I was given. I'm not a remarkable person. I'm not going to be someone who cures cancer, or figures out how to stop climate change. I'm just a guy who knows how to write somewhat decent software. 

But out there somewhere is the first-generation kid who IS going to solve one of those problems. The kid who is TRULY remarkable. I give to MFOS for that kid. Because they deserve the same opportunity that I got - the opportunity to succeed, to make something of themselves. They deserve to be happy. 

Because of this program, I'm in a position to help that happen. It's now that I realize: the MFOS wasn't a godsend. It was sent by people who believed in my potential. And to them, I'm eternally grateful. 

Maybe your life has been impacted by a charity in some similar way. Maybe it hasn't. Either way, I'd encourage you to look into donating. Even when I could only afford to give $20 a month, it brought me nothing but happiness to know I was helping these causes. Even if there's nothing that impacted your life as tangibly as mine, I'm sure there is something you care about. 

Go help out. It'll make you happier.
