The different types of retirement accounts always seem to confuse people, so I wanted to make a quick writeup on the high level of what the differences are between the most common kinds of accounts. 

### IRA and 401k

There are two main kinds of retirement account that most people working for a for-profit company are likely to see: a 401k and an IRA. 

The difference between these is pretty simple: a 401k is sponsored by your employer, but an IRA is something you create and sponsor yourself. There's a couple of other distinctions between them, but this is the primary difference, and all we need to think about right now. We'll cover the finer points below. 

### Traditonal and Roth

There are two main classes of retirement account: Roth accounts and Traditional accounts. The primary distinction between these is when you are taxed: with a Roth account, you are taxed in the year you make the contribution (so, the present day), but with a Traditional account, you are taxed when you withdraw the money in retirement. Again, we'll cover the finer points below. 

### The intersection

These two things are combined to create a total of 4 possible account types: 

- Traditional 401k (Employer sponsored, taxed upon withdrawal)
- Roth 401k (Employer sponsored, taxed upon contribution)
- Traditional IRA (Self-sponsored, taxed upon withdrawal)
- Roth IRA (Self-sponsored, taxed upon contribution)

Most employers offer only a Traditional 401k, but some offer both a Traditional and a Roth 401k. 

When setting up an IRA at a brokerage, you'll be asked whether you want a Traditional or a Roth IRA. 

(If you're looking for a brokerage, I use and recommend [Betterment](www.betterment.com), as they automate most of the work for you)

### Contribution Limits

The IRS allows you to contribute only a certain amount per year to these types of "tax advantaged" accounts. As of 2021, the contribution limits are $19,500 for 401k accounts, and $6,000 for IRA accounts. If you're older than 50, you can contribute an additional $6,500 to 401k accounts, and an additional $1,000 to IRA accounts. 

These maximums are across all accounts. If I have a Roth and a Traditional IRA, I can contribute $6,000 total for the year, in any proportion I like. I can put it all in my Roth, all in my Traditional, or put $3,000 in each account. 

401k accounts have the same rules, just with a higher contribution limit. 

### When to use each account

This depends on your goals, of course, and all comes with the caveat that I have no certifications in any of this stuff. 

Generally speaking, you want to use Roth accounts when your income is lower, and you expect to make more in the future. Roth accounts are great for younger folks who have a long time before they'll be withdrawing. The longer the money sits and grows, the more gains you'll have that you don't have to pay taxes on. 

If you're NOT planning on contributing the maximum amount, I tend to fund my IRAs first. Since I can invest in anything in an IRA, I can invest in whatever I like, rather than the handful of adequate investments available in the 401k plan chosen by my employer
